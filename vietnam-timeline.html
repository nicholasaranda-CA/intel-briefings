From France's colonial reassertion in 1946 to Nixon's Vietnamization program and the Paris Peace Accords of 1973, the Vietnam conflict unfolded across three decades, four American presidencies, and two distinct wars. What began as an anticolonial insurgency became the defining test of Cold War containment doctrine — and, ultimately, its limits. The maps below orient the geography; the timeline traces the arc from partition to withdrawal.
Indochina After 1954

Geneva Accords partition Vietnam at the 17th Parallel. North Vietnam (DRV) under Ho Chi Minh; South Vietnam (RVN) under U.S.-backed government. Laos and Cambodia nominally neutral.

Vietnam War, 1964–1975

Ho Chi Minh Trail (NVA supply routes through Laos/Cambodia), U.S./ARVN movements, Tet Offensive sites, Invasion of Cambodia (1970), and the 17th Parallel DMZ. Key battles at Hue, Saigon, and My Lai marked.

Gulf of Tonkin, August 1964

Maddox attacked August 2 (confirmed). Maddox and Turner Joy reportedly attacked August 4 — almost certainly a phantom contact. LBJ used both incidents to secure the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution.

Phase I — The French War & the Road to Partition

1946–1954
1946

First Indochina War Begins

France attempts to reassert colonial control after Japanese occupation. Ho Chi Minh's Viet Minh — which declared independence September 2, 1945 — launches armed resistance. Full-scale fighting erupts in Hanoi by December 1946.

Truman Administration
Primary Source — Ho Chi Minh, Declaration of Independence, September 2, 1945
"All men are created equal. They are endowed by their Creator with certain inalienable rights; among these are Life, Liberty, and the pursuit of Happiness... Vietnam has the right to be a free and independent country — and in fact it is so already."
Ho Chi Minh deliberately echoed the U.S. Declaration of Independence. He had appealed to Woodrow Wilson at Versailles (1919) and to Truman (1945) — both were ignored. The rhetorical strategy is deliberate: anticolonial nationalism framed in American terms, aimed at an American audience.
1950

U.S. Backs France; Cold War Stakes Clarify

Truman authorizes $10 million in military aid to France — the first direct U.S. involvement. China and the USSR recognize the DRV. Washington now fears a communist arc from the Soviet Union to Southeast Asia, reframing a colonial war as a Cold War conflict.

Truman Administration
1953–54

Battle of Dien Bien Phu — France Defeated

French forces garrison the remote valley of Dien Bien Phu, expecting to lure the Viet Minh into conventional battle. General Võ Nguyên Giáp hauls artillery over mountains and achieves surrounding fire superiority. After a 57-day siege, France surrenders on May 7, 1954 — the decisive end of French Indochina. Eisenhower declines to intervene militarily.

Eisenhower Administration — Declines Intervention
July 1954

Geneva Accords — Vietnam Divided at 17th Parallel

International conference produces a ceasefire and temporary partition. North Vietnam: Ho Chi Minh (DRV). South Vietnam: Ngo Dinh Diem, backed by the United States. Nationwide elections scheduled for 1956. The U.S. refuses to sign. Diem, knowing Ho Chi Minh would win, refuses to hold the elections. The partition becomes permanent.

Eisenhower Administration

Phase II — Advisors, Diem, and the Slide to War

1955–1963
1955–1960

Diem's Republic & the NLF Forms

Eisenhower sends $1.6 billion in aid and deploys Military Assistance Advisory Group (MAAG) advisors. Diem is Catholic in a Buddhist majority country and governs with authoritarian repression. By 1960, southern communist resisters form the National Liberation Front (NLF) — called "Viet Cong" by Saigon — to wage insurgency against the government.

Eisenhower Administration
1961–1963

Kennedy Escalates Advisors to 16,000

Kennedy inherits ~900 military advisors and expands to 16,000. Special Forces ("Green Berets") deployed. The Strategic Hamlet Program attempts to isolate the population from Viet Cong — and fails. Buddhist Crisis (1963): Diem's government fires on protesters; monk Thích Quảng Đức self-immolates. Kennedy signals willingness to support a leadership change.

Kennedy Administration
Nov. 1963

Coup Against Diem & Kennedy Assassinated

South Vietnamese generals, with tacit U.S. approval via Ambassador Henry Cabot Lodge, overthrow Ngo Dinh Diem. He is murdered. Three weeks later, President Kennedy is assassinated in Dallas. Lyndon Johnson inherits a deteriorating situation — and a set of advisors pressing for escalation.

Kennedy → Johnson

Phase III — Gulf of Tonkin & Full Escalation

1964–1967
August 1964

Gulf of Tonkin Resolution — The Pivotal Deception

USS Maddox is attacked by North Vietnamese torpedo boats on August 2 (confirmed). A second "attack" on August 4 is reported but almost certainly did not occur — weather, darkness, and panicked sonar operators likely produced false contacts. Johnson uses both incidents to secure near-unanimous Congressional authorization. Only Senators Wayne Morse and Ernest Gruening vote no.

Johnson Administration
Primary Source — Gulf of Tonkin Resolution, August 7, 1964 (Public Law 88-408)
"The Congress approves and supports the determination of the President, as Commander in Chief, to take all necessary measures to repel any armed attack against the forces of the United States and to prevent further aggression... [including] all necessary steps, including the use of armed force."
This resolution — based in part on a probable non-event — served as the legal basis for over 500,000 American troops in Vietnam and years of sustained bombing campaigns. Congress formally repealed it in 1971. The resolution is the clearest illustration in American history of how manufactured or misread intelligence can trigger open-ended war powers.
1965

Operation Rolling Thunder & First Ground Combat

Operation Rolling Thunder begins in March: three-year sustained bombing campaign against North Vietnam. First combat troops land at Da Nang in March. By year's end, 184,000 U.S. troops are in-country. General Westmoreland's "search and destroy" strategy aims to inflict unsustainable attrition on the Viet Cong and NVA.

Johnson Administration
1965–1967

Half a Million Troops — and Doubt Grows

Troop levels surge to 485,000 by 1967. Casualties mount. Defense Secretary McNamara privately doubts the war's winability by 1967 (later revealed in the Pentagon Papers). The anti-war movement grows on college campuses. Johnson faces an impossible choice: his Great Society domestic agenda and the Vietnam War are fiscally and politically incompatible.

Johnson Administration

Phase IV — Tet Offensive & the Collapse of Consensus

1968
Jan. 30, 1968

Tet Offensive Launches

On the Vietnamese lunar New Year, NVA and Viet Cong forces launch simultaneous attacks on over 100 cities and towns — including the U.S. Embassy compound in Saigon. Militarily, the offensive fails; attackers are driven back with enormous losses. Psychologically, it is catastrophic for the Johnson administration. Americans had been told the war was being won.

Johnson Administration
Feb.–Mar. 1968

Cronkite, Credibility Gap, and LBJ's Exit

CBS anchor Walter Cronkite broadcasts from Vietnam and declares the war a "stalemate." Johnson reportedly says: "If I've lost Cronkite, I've lost Middle America." On March 31, Johnson announces he will not seek re-election and halts bombing above the 20th Parallel to restart negotiations. The political casualty of Tet is LBJ himself.

Johnson Administration
Primary Source — President Johnson, Address to the Nation, March 31, 1968
"With America's sons in the fields far away, with America's future under challenge right here at home... I do not believe that I should devote an hour or a day of my time to any personal partisan causes or to any duties other than the awesome duties of this office — the Presidency of your country. Accordingly, I shall not seek, and I will not accept, the nomination of my party for another term as your President."
Public Papers of the Presidents of the United States: Lyndon B. Johnson, 1968–69. Johnson's withdrawal was one of the most consequential political moments of the 20th century — forced by a war his administration had built, and an offensive his generals had failed to predict.

Phase V — Vietnamization & the Long Withdrawal

1969–1973
1969

Nixon's Vietnamization Policy

Nixon announces "Vietnamization" — phased withdrawal of U.S. combat troops paired with accelerated training of ARVN forces. The policy acknowledges the war cannot be won militarily, but attempts to shift the burden to South Vietnam while preserving the appearance of non-defeat. Simultaneously, Nixon authorizes the secret bombing of Cambodia (Operation Menu).

Nixon Administration
April–May 1970

Cambodia Incursion & Kent State

Nixon announces U.S.-ARVN ground invasion of Cambodia to destroy NVA sanctuaries. The announcement triggers the largest student strike in U.S. history. On May 4, National Guard troops fire on protesters at Kent State University — killing four students. The incursion deepens anti-war sentiment and accelerates Congressional efforts to limit presidential war powers.

Nixon Administration
1971

Pentagon Papers Published

The New York Times and Washington Post publish the "Pentagon Papers" — a classified Defense Department study leaked by Daniel Ellsberg showing systematic government deception about the war's progress across multiple administrations. Nixon seeks an injunction; the Supreme Court rules 6–3 for the newspapers (New York Times Co. v. United States). Public trust in government collapses further.

Nixon Administration
Jan. 27, 1973

Paris Peace Accords

Henry Kissinger (U.S.) and Lê Đức Thọ (North Vietnam) sign a ceasefire. All U.S. combat troops withdraw within 60 days. South Vietnam's government remains in place — but the NVA stays in the south. Both are awarded the Nobel Peace Prize; Thọ declines. The agreement is a strategic fig leaf: fighting continues. South Vietnam will fall in April 1975.

Nixon Administration
April 30, 1975

Fall of Saigon — Epilogue

Two years after the last U.S. combat troops depart, North Vietnamese forces enter Saigon. Helicopter evacuations from the U.S. Embassy rooftop become the defining image of the war's end. Vietnam is reunified under communist rule (1976). The outcome U.S. policy spent twenty years and 58,000 American lives trying to prevent arrives within two years of withdrawal.

Principal Parties & Actors

All Phases

France

Colonial power in Indochina, 1887–1954. Fought to reassert control after WWII Japanese occupation. Defeated at Dien Bien Phu; withdrew under Geneva Accords.

North Vietnam (DRV)

Ho Chi Minh's Democratic Republic. Led by the Vietnamese Workers' Party. Goal: reunification under socialism. Backed materially by China and the USSR.

South Vietnam (RVN)

U.S.-backed Republic of Vietnam. Governments: Diem → military juntas → Thieu. ARVN forces fought alongside U.S. troops; collapsed without direct U.S. support.

United States

Containment doctrine drove involvement from Truman's aid to France through Nixon's withdrawal. Peak: 543,000 troops (1969). Total deaths: 58,220.

NLF / Viet Cong

National Liberation Front — southern communist insurgents. Waged guerrilla war in the south. Suffered enormous losses at Tet but ultimately prevailed politically.

North Vietnamese Army

Regular NVA forces moved south via the Ho Chi Minh Trail through Laos and Cambodia. Increasingly bore the conventional war burden after Tet destroyed Viet Cong strength.

China (PRC)

Recognized and armed the DRV. Sent ~320,000 support troops to North Vietnam. Fear of Chinese intervention, as in Korea (1950), critically constrained U.S. strategy throughout.

Soviet Union

Provided sophisticated air defenses, MiG fighters, and artillery. Estimated $2 billion in military aid to North Vietnam. The war was, in part, a Cold War proxy conflict.
