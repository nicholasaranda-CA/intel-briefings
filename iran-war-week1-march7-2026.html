Situation Overview

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury — a massive joint air campaign targeting Iran's nuclear infrastructure, ballistic missile arsenal, air defense systems, and senior leadership. The opening strikes, totaling approximately 900 US sorties and 500 Israeli strikes in the first twelve hours, succeeded in killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, the top of the Islamic Republic's command structure, along with dozens of senior military and intelligence officials. CENTCOM stated four strategic objectives: halt Iran's nuclear enrichment, destroy its ballistic missile production capacity, degrade its naval capabilities, and curtail its proxy network. Trump publicly confirmed the broader aim of regime change.

Iran responded immediately with hundreds of ballistic missiles and thousands of one-way attack drones targeting US military bases across nine countries — Bahrain, Iraq, Jordan, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and vessels at sea — as well as Israeli population centers. As of March 7 (Day 8), US and Israeli strikes have reportedly hit over 3,000 targets across 26 of Iran's 31 provinces. The US Institute for the Study of War (ISW) assesses that coalition operations have focused on three priorities: suppressing Iranian air defenses, degrading Iran's capacity to retaliate, and disrupting Iranian command and control. The campaign is the largest combined use of US airpower since Operation Shock and Awe in 2003.

The human and financial costs are escalating rapidly. At least 1,332 Iranian civilians have been confirmed killed, including 181 children. Six US service members have been killed in action, with 18 seriously wounded. The first 100 hours of Operation Epic Fury alone cost an estimated $3.7 billion — approximately $891 million per day, the majority of which was not pre-budgeted. The Strait of Hormuz has been declared closed by Iran, driving Brent crude to $90 per barrel. The conflict has displaced more than 330,000 people across the region and triggered mass civilian evacuations from at least 14 countries. Iran has issued back-channel signals through another nation's intelligence service indicating potential openness to ceasefire terms — though the destruction of Iran's own leadership infrastructure has made it unclear who can actually enforce a deal.

Russian & Chinese Involvement: The Shadow War
Russia: Active Intelligence Support

The Washington Post and CNN independently confirmed on March 6 that Russia is providing Iran with satellite imagery of US warship movements, aircraft positions, and radar system locations. The data originates from Russia's sophisticated overhead satellite constellation — including the Khayyam/Kanopus-V satellite, a joint Russian-Iranian project that provides 1.2-meter high-resolution imagery capable of monitoring specific US and Israeli bases.

US analysts note that Iran's targeting has become markedly more precise since the opening days. Iranian strikes are now more concentrated on command and control nodes — radar sites and communication infrastructure — rather than broad area targets. This doctrinal shift mirrors Russia's own drone campaign in Ukraine: swarms of drones disrupt air defenses, followed by ballistic missile strikes. Defense Secretary Hegseth told CBS that the US is "tracking everything" regarding Russian support.

Prior to the war, Russia delivered S-400 air defense components to Iran and began delivering Su-35 "Flanker-E" jets (48-unit order, est. $6.5B), equipped with Khibiny-M electronic warfare pods designed specifically to detect stealth aircraft like the F-35. A Rezonans-NE over-the-horizon radar was also provided, capable of tracking stealth targets at long ranges. Russia's military pact with Iran does not require it to enter combat — but its intelligence role constitutes meaningful indirect belligerence.

China: Economic Stakes, Covert Preparation

China has publicly condemned the strikes as a "grave violation" of sovereignty and Iran's right to security. Foreign Minister Wang Yi, in coordination with Russian FM Lavrov, called for an "immediate ceasefire." China's official position demands a return to diplomacy and opposes "regime change" by force. At the UN Security Council, China and Russia convened an emergency session — though no resolution or vote has occurred.

Beneath the diplomatic surface, US intelligence indicates China may be preparing to provide Iran with financial assistance, spare parts, and missile components. A private Chinese firm, Mizarvision, has reportedly been tracking American military movements. Iranian FM Araghchi acknowledged in an NBC interview that Russia and China are "supporting us politically and otherwise," declining to elaborate.

China's core calculation differs from Russia's: it is heavily dependent on Iranian oil and is actively pressuring Tehran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz to protect Chinese energy supply chains. The Belt and Road Initiative relies on the International North-South Transport Corridor through Iran's Bandar Abbas port — which has been struck. "China is more cautious," one US source assessed. "It wants the war to end because it endangers their energy supply." Whether Beijing moves from political support to material support would mark a significant great-power escalation threshold.

⚠ Alert: Great Power Complicity Threshold

The combination of Russian targeting intelligence and potential Chinese material support represents the emergence of a proxy great-power confrontation embedded within the US-Iran war. Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi explicitly confirmed foreign "political and other" support. If China crosses into material resupply — even spare parts or financial transfers — the United States would face a qualitatively different strategic environment: one in which Iranian military persistence is externally subsidized and Iranian targeting is externally enhanced. This threshold has not yet been crossed, but US intelligence assesses it is under active Chinese consideration as of March 6–7.

ISW Operational Status Report — As of March 6, 2026

The Institute for the Study of War assesses Operation Epic Fury has achieved significant kinetic success against three of four stated US objectives while the fourth — regime change — remains structurally elusive under an air campaign alone.

Objective 1: Suppress Air Defenses ✓ LARGELY ACHIEVED
Israel claims 80% of Iranian air defense systems destroyed. The US and Israel established effective control of Iranian airspace from western Iran to central Tehran within 24 hours of Day 1. The initial strike wave targeted approximately 200 air defense systems. Iran's Russian-supplied S-400 components have been rendered largely inoperable.
Objective 2: Degrade Retaliation Capacity ✓ SUBSTANTIAL PROGRESS
Iranian theater ballistic missile launches down 86–90% from Day 1 peak. Drone attacks down 83%. Iran has fired 500+ ballistic missiles and ~2,000 drones total. However, Iran's hit rate per missile fired has increased from 4% to 24% as Russian intelligence improves targeting. Drone swarms continue to penetrate Gulf state defenses.
Objective 3: Disrupt Command & Control ✓ SEVERE DISRUPTION
Khamenei killed. Chief of Staff, Defense Minister, IRGC Commander, head of emergency command intelligence, SPND director, and 7+ senior security leaders confirmed dead. Iran's Foreign Ministry stated "the military has lost control over several units, that are operating according to old general instructions." IRGC hardliners now dominate remaining decision-making — complicating negotiations.
Objective 4: Regime Change ✗ NOT ACHIEVED — STRUCTURALLY UNCERTAIN
Despite decapitation of leadership and unprecedented military degradation, the Islamic Republic's institutional apparatus remains intact. IRGC maintains internal security. Civilian populations are unarmed and unorganized. No evidence of large-scale military defections. ISW and ACLED both assess that air campaigns alone have never achieved regime collapse — and this one is unlikely to be the exception.

⚡ HOTFLASH — Turkey & the Kurdish Dimension

The war has opened a volatile secondary front involving Iranian Kurdish armed groups, Iraqi Kurdish political leaders, and a deeply uncomfortable NATO ally — Turkey — whose interests cut across virtually every actor in the conflict. This dimension is one of the war's highest-risk escalation vectors: a potential ground war inside Iran's western border, fueled by CIA weapons, opposed by Ankara, and complicated by a century of unresolved Kurdish statelessness.

The Kurdish Coalition
Who Is the CPFIK?

Five major Iranian Kurdish opposition parties formally united on February 22 — just six days before the war began — under the Coalition of Political Forces of Iranian Kurdistan (CPFIK): PJAK, PDKI, PAK, the Khabat Organization, and a branch of the Komala Party. Komala of Iranian Kurdistan joined as a sixth member on March 4. The coalition's stated aims are "toppling the Iranian regime" and achieving Kurdish self-determination in Iranian Kurdistan. After eight months of negotiations, the coalition produced a joint command structure for armed wings, a diplomatic committee, and a framework for elections in liberated territory — a degree of organizational sophistication that analysts say rebuts the standard dismissal of Iran's opposition as too fractured to matter.

On March 2, the coalition called on Iranian armed forces in Kurdish regions to "separate themselves from the remnants of the Islamic Republic." PDKI forces confirmed attacks on Iranian military and missile facilities. By March 6, a senior PDKI official stated that "a large force of ours is already in Iran" and that military action would begin "as soon as possible." The groups are publicly awaiting a US "green light."

CIA Arming Operation — Confirmed

CNN confirmed on March 3 that the CIA has been actively arming Iranian Kurdish forces — and that this effort began months before the war. The agency maintains an outpost in Iraqi Kurdistan near the Iran border. ITV News reported that weapons have been smuggled into western Iran since the prior year's Twelve-Day War, arming thousands of Kurdish volunteers.

Trump personally called KDPI president Mustafa Hijri on March 4. He separately called Iraqi Kurdish leaders Masoud Barzani and Bafel Talabani to "discuss the next stages of war." But Barzani and Talabani expressed reservations: Peshmerga involvement risks catastrophic Iranian retaliation against the Kurdistan Region of Iraq. The KRG formally stated that allegations it is part of any arming plan are "completely unfounded." Iran preemptively struck PAK and PDKI bases in Erbil on March 2, killing at least one fighter, and on March 6 threatened to target "all facilities" in the Kurdistan Region if Kurdish militants are permitted to enter Iran.

Turkey's Position — NATO's Uncomfortable Ally
Ankara's Core Interests

Turkey sits at the intersection of every pressure point in this conflict: NATO member, Iran's neighbor, in active PKK peace negotiations, and deeply opposed to a Kurdish-fueled insurgency on its eastern flank. Turkey refused to allow its airspace to be used for strikes on Iran — a meaningful constraint on US logistics. Turkish FM Hakan Fidan told his Iranian counterpart in January, weeks before the war, that "the complete neutralization of PJAK constitutes an urgent necessity for Iran's security."

On March 5, Turkey's Defense Ministry formally stated that groups "fueling ethnic separatism, such as PJAK, negatively affect not only Iran's security but also the overall peace and stability of the region" — a pointed signal directed as much at Washington as at Tehran. Carnegie Endowment senior fellow Alper Coşkun warned that US cooperation with Kurdish actors "hostile to Turkey" risks making the Iran war "the next stress test in US-Turkish relations," potentially unraveling the fragile diplomatic momentum between the two NATO allies.

Iranian Missile Strikes Turkey — Article 4 Invoked

In one of the war's most dangerous moments for alliance cohesion, an Iranian ballistic missile landed in Dörtyol, Hatay Province, Turkey. NATO air defense systems — including Spanish assets at Incirlik — intercepted the missile. NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte reaffirmed the alliance's commitment to Turkey's defense. Iran officially denied intentional targeting, calling it a "technical anomaly."

Turkey invoked NATO Article 4 (consultation on threats to territorial integrity), triggering urgent contacts between FM Fidan and allied leadership. Turkish analysts assess that Iran's intent was to signal Ankara: stop sharing intelligence with the United States or face escalation. Turkey separately condemned Iran's drone strikes on Azerbaijan on March 5. Ankara is now simultaneously managing a missile near its border, a CIA-armed Kurdish proxy force being built by its ally, and domestic public opinion: nearly 60% of Turks favor regime change in Iran — running directly counter to Erdoğan's cautious policy posture.

The PKK Complication

Turkey's deepest anxiety is not Iran — it is the PKK. Ankara is mid-process in negotiations to dissolve and disarm the PKK, a process that could collapse if PJAK (widely seen as the PKK's Iranian branch) launches a high-profile US-backed offensive inside Iran. Turkish analysts warn that a strengthened PJAK presence could revive the PKK as a geopolitical actor and create a "Kurdish belt" stretching from Syria through northern Iraq into western Iran — precisely the scenario Ankara has opposed for decades. The threat is not hypothetical: Trump has reportedly threatened to cut US funding for Kurdish security forces unless they assist in the Iran campaign, potentially forcing Barzani and Talabani into a corner that also pressures Ankara.

Notably, Turkey's own pro-Kurdish party — the DEM Party, the third-largest bloc in parliament — opposed the Kurdish offensive on March 3, calling it a vehicle for "external designs" by global powers rather than a genuine path to Kurdish liberation. Even within Kurdish political culture, the war has produced a split between those who see it as a strategic opportunity and those who see it as great-power manipulation.

⚠ Assessment — The Kurdish Card: High Risk, Uncertain Payoff

The CIA arming operation and potential Kurdish ground offensive represent the Trump administration's bet that territorial pressure from the west can accelerate Iranian regime collapse. The bet is analytically contested on multiple levels. Iran has already demonstrated willingness to strike Kurdish bases in Iraq preemptively. Iraqi Kurdish leaders have explicitly warned that Peshmerga involvement risks catastrophic Iranian retaliation. Turkey — a NATO member the US cannot afford to alienate — has signaled that US cooperation with PJAK-linked forces will damage bilateral relations. The large Iraqi Kurdish parties (KDP and PUK) have declined involvement. And JINSA itself acknowledges the "bitter example" of US abandonment of Kurdish partners in Syria — a recent wound that makes Kurdish commitment to any partnership contingent on credible, sustained US support. The green light has not yet been given. If it is, the conflict acquires a ground dimension no air campaign can manage — and the war's political costs will extend well beyond the Middle East into the NATO alliance itself.

Analysis & Assessment
Near-Term (Days 8–21) — The Kinetic-Diplomatic Race

Operation Epic Fury has succeeded in its immediate military objectives: Iranian airspace is contested, its navy is largely eliminated, its missile launch rate is suppressed by 86-90%, and its senior command structure has been gutted. These are remarkable kinetic achievements. But the war is entering a phase where military success and strategic success diverge. Iran has opened a backchannel to the CIA, but the very strikes that pressured Tehran into signaling have also eliminated most of the Iranian officials Washington would need to negotiate with. Trump acknowledged this directly: "Most of the people we had in mind are dead." The next two to three weeks will determine whether a ceasefire framework can be assembled before the US intercept stockpiles — already stressed after seven days of intensive air defense — become a constraining factor. CSIS estimates initial munitions expenditure at $3.7B in 100 hours; resupply timelines are not publicly known.

Medium-Term (Weeks 3–8) — The Great Power Complication

Russian intelligence support has already improved Iran's targeting precision — demonstrated by a shift from broad-area strikes toward US command, control, and radar infrastructure. If China crosses the threshold into material resupply (missile components, financial transfers), the US faces a qualitatively different adversary: one whose military persistence is externally subsidized. This creates an uncomfortable parallel to the Afghanistan and Vietnam dynamics, where external resupply prolonged conflicts indefinitely. The US has escalation options — sanctioning Chinese firms involved in resupply, direct diplomatic confrontation with Moscow — but these carry risks of broader great-power conflict. The most likely medium-term outcome is a negotiated suspension of hostilities, not a decisive US-Israeli victory, as Iran retains sufficient institutional and territorial integrity to make occupation or forced regime transition untenable.

Long-Term (Months 2+) — The Regime Change Paradox

The dominant analytical framework in Washington appears to assume that sufficient military pressure will either produce regime collapse or a maximally favorable ceasefire. Neither outcome is assured. ACLED and CFR's Richard Haass independently assess that air campaigns alone have never toppled a regime — not in Serbia, not in Libya (where ground-level collapse preceded NATO action), and not in any prior case in modern history. IRGC hardliners now control Iranian decision-making; they are more ideologically committed and less pragmatic than the civilian leadership that was killed. Iran may calculate that it is better positioned to absorb the costs of a long war than the United States, which faces domestic economic pressure (Dow -1,000 on Day 2, oil at $90/barrel), an unbounded military expenditure, and no clear endgame. The most durable long-term outcome consistent with US stated objectives — a non-nuclear, non-missile Iran — requires a successor Iranian government willing to enforce verified disarmament, and no such government yet exists. If one does not emerge organically, the US faces a choice between an indefinite military occupation and a negotiated settlement that preserves some version of the Islamic Republic.

Discussion Questions
  • 01 // Operation Epic Fury achieved significant kinetic results — air defense suppression, naval elimination, and command decapitation — but has not achieved regime change. What does this gap between operational success and strategic success reveal about the limits of military power as a tool of foreign policy?
  • 02 // Russia is providing Iran with satellite targeting intelligence while China assesses whether to supply missile components. Neither has formally entered the conflict. How does this kind of "shadow belligerence" challenge traditional definitions of war — and what obligations, if any, does it create for the United States to respond?
  • 03 // Trump demanded Iran's "unconditional surrender," but acknowledged that most of the Iranian officials the US had "in mind" for negotiations are now dead. What does it mean strategically to destroy your adversary's ability to negotiate? Does military success ever become self-defeating?

What does this tell you about the evolving nature of warfare — and how should the United States think about the vulnerability of its forward-deployed assets in an era of adversary intelligence sharing?
  • 05 // Richard Haass of CFR argues this was a "war of choice," not a "war of necessity," and that the US has not offered convincing evidence of an imminent Iranian threat. Do you find this distinction meaningful in this case? Under what conditions, if any, should states launch preventive military action — and who gets to make that judgment?
