Mr. Aranda  ·  Reading Guide

Reading Is Not One Thing.
Neither Is the Work.

There is a version of reading that takes six minutes a page and a version that takes six seconds. Both are legitimate. Both are skills. The confusion — the reason students either slow-crawl everything or skim past what actually matters — is that nobody teaches you to tell the difference.

This guide is about that distinction. What kind of reading a text demands, how to move through it efficiently, and how to engage it when efficiency stops being the right goal. The research on this is clear. What follows reflects it.

Four Modes of Reading — Not All Reading Is the Same Act

Before you open a text, you should know what you're doing with it. The purpose shapes everything: how fast you move, what you mark, how much you're allowed to skip. Treating every text the same way — slow and passive or fast and careless — is the source of most reading problems.

Skim / Scan
450–600words per minute

Strategic, selective extraction of main ideas. You are not reading every sentence — you are hunting for structure, argument, and signposts. This is a disciplined skill, not a shortcut.

Use when: orienting to a new text, deciding what deserves closer attention, reviewing before class or an exam, surveying sources for research.

Survey / Understand
200–300words per minute

Reading for the meaning of each sentence, following the argument, but not yet interrogating it. You are building comprehension, not yet building a critique.

Use when: reading background material, textbook chapters, news analysis, primary sources you need to understand but not yet challenge.

Study / Engage
90–180words per minute

Annotating as you go — circling terms, tracking arguments, writing reactions, asking questions the author hasn't answered. The margin becomes a workspace.

Use when: reading assigned texts for discussion, preparing for essays, working through dense theoretical or historical material.

Close Reading
60–90words per minute

Intensive attention to a specific passage — word choice, structure, argument, assumptions. You may read the same paragraph three times. That is the point.

Use when: analyzing primary sources, working through a difficult theoretical passage, preparing to write directly from or against a specific text.

Reading Speed — What the Research Actually Says

The optimal reading rate for a skilled adult is around 300 words per minute — for familiar material, reading to understand. Speed reading beyond that reduces comprehension in a near-linear fashion. But skimming for main ideas is a different cognitive task entirely, and the average college student can skim effectively at 450–600 wpm. Speed is not fixed — it is a function of text difficulty, background knowledge, and purpose. Adapted from Rayner et al. (2016) and the Wake Forest Time Estimator.

Skimming for Arguments — A Skill Worth Building

Skimming is not reading in a hurry. It is a disciplined, systematic technique for finding the most information in the least amount of time — and it deserves as much practice as close reading does. A good skimmer is not lazy; they are strategic. The question they're always answering is: where is the argument, and what does it cost me to skip this sentence?

What to Skim For

You are looking for the argument, not the elaboration. Introductions state the claim. Conclusions restate and extend it. First and last sentences of paragraphs carry the load. Section headings telegraph the structure. Bold terms signal what the author thinks matters.

Signal words are your navigation: however, therefore, in contrast, I will argue, this suggests, the central problem. When you see them, slow down. Everything around them is likely elaboration you can move through faster.

When to Slow Down

Skimming is not an excuse to miss the argument. The moment you notice a claim you don't understand, a term you can't define, or a passage that seems to be doing real structural work — that's the signal to stop and read.

The skill is not skimming everything. It's knowing the difference between a paragraph that develops the argument and one that illustrates it. The first demands your attention. The second can often be summarized in a word.

Beginnings & Endings

First and last sentences of paragraphs, first and last paragraphs of sections, introductions and conclusions of chapters. These carry the structural weight. Everything in between is usually support.

Wheat vs. Chaff

Read only enough to determine whether a section presents a main idea or develops one already stated. Main idea: read carefully. Development of something already understood: move through it.

Visual & Verbal Cues

Headings, bold terms, graphs, italicized key words — these are the author's own navigational system. Use it. They mark where the argument concentrates and where it's most efficient to anchor your attention.

Marginalia & Annotation — The Conversation You Have Before Class

Annotation is not note-taking. It is a record of your thinking as it moves through the text — and that thinking is what you bring to discussion. The margin is where you build a position before the room opens. A text you annotated is one you can argue about. A text you only read is one you can describe.

Skimming is a strategic, selective reading method in which you focus on the main ideas of a text. When skimming, deliberately skip text that provides details, stories, data, or other elaboration. Instead of closely reading every word, focus on the introduction, chapter summaries, first and last sentences of paragraphs, bold words, and text features. Skimming is extracting the essence of the author's main messages rather than the finer points.

Circle + Define

Circle terms you can't define. Write the definition in your own words — not the dictionary's. If you can't do it, that's where the gap is.

Underline the Argument

Underline load-bearing sentences: the thesis, key claims, moments where the author commits to something. Not every sentence. The ones the rest depends on.

Record Your Reactions

Skeptical? Say so. Surprised? Note why. Confused? Mark it and keep moving. These are the raw materials for class. Don't wait until discussion to have a response.

Summarize Each Section

At the end of a section, close the text and write two sentences: what the author argued and whether you find it persuasive. If you can't, go back.

Track the Sequence

Number the author's moves in the margin — claim, evidence, objection, response. The order of an argument matters as much as any individual part of it.

Mark the Gaps

Where is the evidence thin? What assumption is doing work the author never defends? What would a critic say here? The margin is where your counter-argument starts.

For Academic Papers — Keshav's Three-Pass Method

Applies specifically to research papers and dense scholarly articles. The logic: don't plow from beginning to end. Read in passes, each with a distinct goal, each building on the last. A first pass takes ten minutes and tells you whether the second pass is worth your time. That's not skimming carelessly — it's resource allocation.

Pass One — Orient

Title, abstract, headings, conclusion only. Answer: what kind of argument is this, what's the central claim, and is it worth your time?

5–10 minutes
Pass Two — Follow

Read carefully, skip deep technical weeds. Take margin notes. Examine figures. Goal: explain the argument to someone else from memory.

Up to 1 hour
Pass Three — Take Apart

Challenge every assumption. Find where the evidence is thin. Ask what a rigorous critic would say. This is where understanding becomes judgment.

1–5 hours
