◆ Key Judgment — The Big Picture
On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched the largest joint military operation in decades against the Islamic Republic of Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei and dozens of senior commanders. As of March 3, the war is in its fourth day. Thousands of targets have been struck across Iran; Iran has responded by firing missiles and drones at Israel, US military bases across the Middle East, and Gulf Arab states. This is not simply a military event — it is the culmination of years of escalating conflict, a collapsed nuclear deal, a domestic uprising inside Iran, and a deliberate strategic choice by the Trump administration to pursue regime change rather than diplomacy.
▸ Situation Overview — What Is Actually Happening

For most of 2025, Iran and Israel were already at war — quietly. Israel destroyed much of Iran's air defense system and damaged its nuclear facilities in June 2025 (Operation Midnight Hammer). Iran's economy was collapsing, its currency nearly worthless, and its people furious. In late December 2025, massive protests erupted across more than 100 Iranian cities — the largest uprising since the 1979 Islamic Revolution. On January 8, 2026, Iranian security forces carried out one of the deadliest single-day crackdowns in the Islamic Republic's history. Thousands of civilians were killed in the streets. The US and Israel watched — and planned.

After indirect nuclear talks in February 2026 collapsed — Iran was reportedly close to a deal, but the US demanded a complete halt to all uranium enrichment — President Trump authorized the assault. In the early hours of February 28, waves of US B-2 bombers, Israeli jets, and Tomahawk cruise missiles struck Tehran, Isfahan, Qom, Karaj, and Kermanshah. Khamenei was killed in the first hours. Iran immediately retaliated, firing ballistic missiles and drone swarms at Israel, Bahrain, UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and Jordan — targeting US military bases, airports, and oil infrastructure. By March 3 the war has produced thousands of casualties, a global travel crisis as over 1,900 flights are canceled, oil prices near a one-year high, and a dangerous vacuum at the top of the Iranian government.

▸ What the US & Israel Say They Want

In an 8-minute video posted at 2:30 AM on February 28, Trump declared the operation's goals: destroy Iran's missile capabilities, prevent nuclear weapons development, and — most dramatically — topple the Iranian regime. He told the Iranian public: "Take over your government. It will be yours to take." Netanyahu echoed this, calling the campaign a chance to free Iranians from tyranny.

This goes beyond any US military action in the Middle East in recent memory. The US did not just target nuclear sites — it targeted the people and institutions running Iran: the Supreme Leader, military commanders, the secret police, and the Law Enforcement Command (the force that crushed the January protesters).

▸ What Iran Says & What It Has Done

Iran's surviving leaders — security chief Larijani and parliament speaker Ghalibaf — have vowed revenge. Iran declared 40 days of national mourning for Khamenei. A temporary three-person leadership council has been formed under its constitution. Iran launched over 330 drones and ballistic missiles at Gulf states in the first two days alone.

Iran's IRGC threatened to close the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow chokepoint through which roughly 20% of the world's oil passes — and attacked commercial oil tankers in the region. As of March 3, six US service members have been confirmed killed, the US Embassy in Riyadh was struck by drones, and both US Embassies in Saudi Arabia and Kuwait have closed.

Operation Midnight Hammer: US and Israel strike Iran's nuclear facilities. Iran loses much of its air defense. Nuclear program set back but not destroyed. Iran immediately begins rebuilding.

Massive protests erupt across 100+ Iranian cities. Driven by economic collapse and repression. The largest uprising since 1979. Protesters call for regime change, wave pre-revolution flags.

Operations Epic Fury & Roaring Lion begin. US and Israeli strikes hit Tehran, Isfahan, Qom, Karaj, Kermanshah. Supreme Leader Khamenei killed in opening hours. 40+ senior officials killed. Iran launches immediate retaliatory strikes across the region.

Iran strikes US Embassy in Riyadh. US closes embassies in Saudi Arabia & Kuwait. 1,900+ flights canceled; 1M+ travelers stranded. Dow Jones drops 1,000+ points. Iran reportedly blocks Strait of Hormuz. War enters Day 4.

◆ Why Does This Map Matter?
The LEC is not a battlefield military unit — it is Iran's internal police force, responsible for keeping the population in line. By systematically striking provincial LEC headquarters across all 31 provinces, the US and Israel are not just degrading Iran's ability to fight back abroad. They are dismantling the apparatus that lets the regime survive at home. Without police forces willing to suppress protesters, a government like Iran's cannot endure a popular uprising. This map is direct visual evidence that the stated goal — regime change — is being pursued by targeting the institutions that protect the regime from its own people.

⚠ Critical Context — What Is the Strait of Hormuz?
The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which roughly 20% of all the world's oil passes every day. Iran has threatened to close it — and has already attacked commercial tankers transiting the area. If Iran successfully disrupts shipping through the Strait, global oil prices would spike dramatically, raising the cost of gasoline, food, and goods worldwide. Countries with no involvement in the war would feel the consequences. This is one of the most powerful economic weapons Iran possesses. The Dow Jones Industrial Average has already dropped over 1,000 points in a single trading day since the war began.

▸ Why This Moment Is Historically Significant
① The Regime Change Doctrine
The US has not openly pursued the military overthrow of a foreign government since the 2003 Iraq invasion — and that ended in a two-decade quagmire. Operation Epic Fury is explicitly regime change. If it succeeds, it rewrites the rules of international conflict. If it produces chaos, it will define the next generation of foreign policy debate in the US and beyond.
② The Nuclear Question Isn't Over
Iran was reportedly days from a nuclear deal when the strikes began. The IAEA reports no nuclear installations have been hit or damaged in this campaign. If Iran's government collapses, who controls remaining nuclear materials? This is the most serious proliferation risk of the conflict — not whether Iran builds a bomb, but whether components end up in unknown hands amid the chaos of a collapsing state.
③ A New Middle East Is Being Born
Iran's proxy network — Hezbollah, Hamas, Houthis, Iraqi militias — was built over 40 years. The June 2025 war weakened it; this war may shatter it. But a power vacuum in Lebanon, Iraq, Syria, and Gaza doesn't automatically produce stability. Regional powers like Turkey and Saudi Arabia are already maneuvering to shape what comes next.
▸ Where Do We Go From Here — Three Scenarios

Scenario A — Regime Collapses / Rapid End

Trump projected the operation would last 4–5 weeks. The intended outcome: Iranian security forces stop fighting, the population rises, and a new Iranian government emerges. CTP-ISW's March 2 report notes early signs — some Iranian commanders are not reporting to their bases, suggesting fractures in the IRGC's chain of command. Trump told The Atlantic "things are evolving in a very positive way." The key unknown: is there a credible, organized opposition inside Iran ready to step into power?

Scenario B — Grinding Regional War

Iran's regime survives in degraded form. The IRGC keeps fighting. Hezbollah escalates in Lebanon. Iraqi militias expand attacks on US forces. The war stretches well past 4–5 weeks, risking direct confrontation with other regional actors. Oil prices stay elevated. Global economic disruption intensifies. This scenario echoes the aftermath of the 2003 Iraq invasion — a regime removed but years of instability left behind.

Scenario C — Negotiated Off-Ramp

Trump has signaled openness. He told The Atlantic: "They want to talk, and I have agreed to talk." Iran's surviving leadership has also sent signals it may negotiate — but many of the people who would have led those talks have been killed. Oman, which mediated the February talks, remains a potential back-channel. A ceasefire followed by a new political settlement is possible, but it would require Iran's surviving leaders to accept terms they rejected before the bombs fell.

⚠ The Hardest Question
The US has declared its goal is regime change — but no public plan exists for what comes after. Iran is a country of 87 million people, the Middle East's second-largest by population. Who governs if the Islamic Republic falls? History offers a warning: removing a regime is the easy part. Filling the vacuum is what determines whether the outcome is liberation or chaos. Iraq in 2003 and Libya in 2011 are the precedents analysts keep citing — and neither ended well.
▸ Discussion Questions — Think, Don't Just Recall
  • 1.Iran was reportedly within days of signing a nuclear deal when the strikes began. If that's true, why do you think the US and Israel chose military action instead of waiting for an agreement? What does this tell you about how the Trump administration weighs diplomatic solutions against military force?
  • 2.The US-Israeli strikes specifically targeted Iran's Law Enforcement Command (LEC) — the police force that crushed the January protests. What does it mean strategically to target a government's internal police apparatus rather than just its military? How does this connect to the stated goal of regime change?
  • 3.Russia and China have condemned the strikes but are not intervening militarily. Why might powerful countries that consider themselves Iran's partners choose words over action? What does this tell us about the limits of alliances built on shared interests rather than shared values or treaty obligations?
  • 4.Iran is striking airports, ports, and oil infrastructure in Gulf Arab states that had no direct role in the US-Israeli attack. What strategic logic is behind this? Who gets hurt, and what message is Iran trying to send to the broader region and the world?
  • 5.The US pursued regime change in Iraq (2003) and Libya (2011). Both regimes fell — but both countries descended into prolonged instability. Based on what you've read in this briefing, what conditions would need to be true for Iran to have a different outcome? Is there reason to believe those conditions exist?
